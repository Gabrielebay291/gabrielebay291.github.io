About

Trustworthy AI, from principle to deployment

I earned my M.S. in Data Science at Fordham University — with a thesis on the interpretability and explainability of large language models — and am now pursuing my Ph.D. in the Department of Computer & Information Science. My guiding question is simple but urgent: as AI systems become more autonomous, how do we keep them safe, accountable, and worthy of human trust?

My work moves across three fronts — evaluating and hardening LLM moderation and safety, designing agentic AI systems for healthcare, and building interpretable deep-learning models for medical imaging — always with transparency and human-first design at the center.

Research Focus

What I work on

Four intersecting threads, unified by a commitment to trustworthy and transparent AI.

🛡️

AI Safety & LLM Moderation

Benchmarks and moderation models that evaluate the safety, fairness, and adversarial robustness of LLMs — advocating a human-first approach.

🤖

Agentic AI for Healthcare

Autonomous, tool-using LLM agents that can reason and act reliably in high-stakes clinical and medical settings.

🔍

Interpretability & Explainability

Making model decisions transparent — from contextual representations over large knowledge bases to explainable LLM behavior.

🧬

Deep Learning for Medical Imaging

3D segmentation (nnU-Net, SAM2) and radiomics for knee & hip MRI — from osteoarthritis progression to femoroacetabular impingement.

✦ Current research spotlight

Agentic AI you can actually trust

My most recent work sits at the frontier of agentic AI — LLM-driven systems that plan, use tools, and act autonomously. The hard part isn't capability; it's accountability. Through projects like RA-Gent and the GuardEval benchmark, I study how to evaluate and guardrail autonomous agents so that safety, fairness, and robustness scale alongside their autonomy — with a special focus on healthcare, where the cost of an unreliable agent is highest.
