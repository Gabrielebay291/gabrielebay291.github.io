About

I study how to make modern AI systems trustworthy. As large language models and autonomous agents take on more responsibility, my work asks a practical question: how do we keep them safe, fair, and transparent enough for people to rely on?

I finished my MS in Data Science at Fordham University, where my thesis looked at the interpretability and explainability of large language models. I am now a PhD researcher in the Department of Computer & Information Science. Day to day, my research moves between three areas: evaluating and hardening LLM moderation and safety, building agentic AI for healthcare, and designing interpretable deep learning models for medical imaging.

Agentic AI (current focus)

What I am working on now

My most recent work sits with agentic AI, the LLM driven systems that plan, call tools, and act on their own. The hard part is not raw capability; it is accountability. Through projects like RA‑Gent and the GuardEval benchmark, I study how to evaluate and guardrail autonomous agents so that safety, fairness, and robustness keep pace with their autonomy. Healthcare is my main testing ground, because that is where an unreliable agent does the most harm.

Research interests

AI safety & LLM moderation
Benchmarks and moderation models that test the safety, fairness, and adversarial robustness of LLMs, with a human first approach.
Agentic AI for healthcare
Autonomous, tool using LLM agents that reason and act reliably in clinical and medical settings.
Interpretability & explainability
Making model decisions legible, from contextual representations over large knowledge bases to explainable LLM behavior.
Deep learning for medical imaging
3D segmentation (nnU-Net, SAM2) and radiomics for knee and hip MRI, including osteoarthritis progression and femoroacetabular impingement.
